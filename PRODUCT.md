# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Hiring managers, recruiters, and senior product designers at fintech and enterprise SaaS companies in the US, evaluating Priyamwada for AI product design roles. They land here from a resume link or LinkedIn, spend under two minutes per case study, and are scanning for craft quality, clarity of thinking, and range, not reading exhaustively. Some are technical (engineers, technical PMs) and will notice if the site itself is slow or sloppily built.

Job to be done: decide, fast, whether this person's design thinking and execution are strong enough to warrant an interview.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio that doubles as a work sample. Every page is both content (case studies, experiments) and proof (the site's own speed, craft, and interaction design demonstrate the skills being claimed). Success looks like a hiring manager finishing a case study in under two minutes with a clear read on the problem, the designer's reasoning, and the outcome, then wanting to see more.

## Brand Personality

Curious, playful, sharp. Confident but not hyped; plain-spoken and specific rather than corporate or vague. First-person and casual in voice ("I tinker," "exercise my design muscles") without undercutting technical credibility, real metrics are cited directly (e.g. "cut manual work by 70%"). Playfulness shows up in the creative-coding side quests (ASCII game, WebGL shaders, a 1984-Mac-style GUI reimagining) and should read as genuine tinkering, not decoration bolted onto a serious portfolio.

## Anti-references

- Generic template feel: anything that reads as an off-the-shelf Webflow/Framer theme with no distinctive point of view.
- Slow, heavy sites: the previous Framer-built version of this portfolio lagged badly enough that a senior design director said he'd have closed the tab if he didn't already know her. Speed is a first-impression requirement, not a nice-to-have.
- Text-heavy, slow-to-parse case studies: prior feedback from three senior designers independently flagged the case studies as too long and too dense.
- Framing AI/conversational work as "chatbot design": this reads as trivial. Reframe as complex workflow and decision-making design.
- Positioning as mid-level: prior India-based experience does not read as mid-level in the US market. Lead with craft quality and learning velocity, not years of experience.

## Design Principles

1. **The site is a work sample.** Every interaction, animation, and load time is itself evidence of design and craft ability, not just a container for case study content.
2. **Show complexity before showing UI.** Case studies should establish the problem's real difficulty (a framework visual, decision matrix, or system map) before showing screens, so the work doesn't read as simpler than it was.
3. **Get to the work fast.** Assume a two-minute attention budget per case study. Cut ruthlessly; lead with outcome and reasoning, not exhaustive process narration.
4. **Confidence through specificity, not volume.** Real numbers and plain statements build more credibility than superlatives or lengthy justification.
5. **Play is part of the pitch.** The creative-coding experiments are not filler, they demonstrate range and genuine curiosity, and should be held to the same craft bar as the case studies.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AAA where feasible (higher bar than the WCAG AA default, given this site functions as a professional work sample and is scrutinized by a technical audience). Support reduced-motion preferences given the site's heavy use of animation (GSAP, Framer Motion successor, Lenis smooth scroll) and interactive experiments (WebGL shaders, cursor effects).
